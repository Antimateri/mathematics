# Introduction

Algebraic K-theory is a branch of mathematics that studies algebraic structures with a ring representation using techniques from homological algebra. conceptually it can be seen as the study of certain kinds of invariants of matrices over a ring but its main interests resides in its applications on fields such as:
- Algebraic geometry
- Algebraic topology
- Operator theory
- High energy physics
- ...

Our interest resides in its applications to algebraic topology and as such this post will have definitios and techniques meant to be used in that field even if they are generic enough to be interesting even if topology is not the intended application.

# The projective class group.
We start defining $K_0(R)$, the projective class group of a ring $R$, and studing its properties and how to calculate it.

## Definition and calculation

{{< mathdef type="Definition" name="Projective Group" >}}
Let $R$ be a ring. We define $K_0(R)$ as the abelian group generated by the isomorphism classes of finitely generated projective $R$-modules and with the group operation defined as follows:

for every short exact sequence of finitely generated projective $R$-modules
$$0 \to P_0 \to P_1 \to P_2 \to 0$$
we have the relation $[P_1] = [P_0] + [P_2]$.
{{< /mathdef >}}

The definition of the sum in $K_0(R)$ may seem a bit strange at first but due to the projective nature of the modules we have that every short exact sequence of finitely generated projective $R$-modules splits and thus $P_1 \cong P_0 \oplus P_2$ so the relation can be seen as $[P_0 \oplus P_2] = [P_0] + [P_2]$ which is more intuitive.

Notice $K_0(-)$ defines a functor from the category of rings to the category of abelian groups by sending a ring homomorphism $f: R \to S$ to the group homomorphism 
$$\begin{aligned}
K_0(f): K_0(R) &\to K_0(S)\\\\
[P] &\mapsto [f_*P]=[P \otimes_R S]
\end{aligned}$$
 which would be the natural way for $f$ to send the module $P$ to a module over $S$.

I will let the reader check that this is a well-defined group homomorphism and that $K_0$ is indeed a functor whose end category is abelian groups (that is, check that $K_0(R)$ is an abelian group).

It may be more usefull to work with the universal property of $K_0$ sometimes:

{{< mathdef type="Proposition" name="Universal property of $K_0$" >}}
Let $R$ be a ring and let $G$ be an abelian group. Then, for every function $f$ from the set of isomorphism classes of finitely generated projective $R$-modules to $G$ such that for every short exact sequence of finitely generated projective $R$-modules
$$0 \to P_0 \to P_1 \to P_2 \to 0$$
we have $f([P_1]) = f([P_0]) + f([P_2])$, there exists a unique group homomorphism $\tilde{f}: K_0(R) \to G$ such that $\tilde{f}([P]) = f([P])$ for every finitely generated projective $R$-module $P$.
{{< /mathdef >}}

As implied in the begining, $K$-theory is interested in invariants of matrices over a ring a first aproximattio to this is the following result:

{{< mathdef type="Theorem" name="Morita Equivalence for algebraic K-theory" >}}
For every ring $R$, every $m \in \mathbb{N}$, and every $n \in \mathbb{Z}$, we have
$$K_n(R) \cong K_n(M_m(R))$$
{{< /mathdef >}}

We will dedefine the higher and negative $K$-groups later but for now we can use this result to see that $K_0(R) \cong K_0(M_m(R))$ and thus we can work with matrix groups instead of modules if we want to calculate $K_0(R)$.

### Calculation

To start, lets get an intuition for what a finitely generated projective module is. A finitely generated $R$-module $M$ is projective if and only if $M$ is "locally free", that is, for all $p \in \mathrm{Spec}(R)$, the localization $M_p$ is a free $R_p$-module.

 If $R$ is a field, then it has no non-trivial ideals, so every locally free module is free, in particular every finitely generated projective $R$-module is free, and as such, isomorphic to $R^n$ for some $n \in \mathbb{N}$ so we have that $K_0(R) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ with the isomorphism given by $[R^n] \mapsto n$.

Actually, to have that $K_0(R) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ it is enough for $R$ to be a principal ideal domain. This follows from the structure theorem of finitely generated $R$-modules over principal ideal domains and the split property of finitely generated projective $R$-modules.

{{< mathdef type="Lemma" name="$K_0$ of PID" >}}
Let $R$ be a principal ideal domain, then $K_0(R) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ with the isomorphism given by $[R^n] \mapsto n$.
{{< /mathdef >}}

This next lemma will be very usefull to calculate the projective class.

{{< mathdef type="Lemma" name="$K_n$ of product rings" >}}
Let $R_0$ and $R_1$ be rings, then for all $n\in \mathbb{Z}$
$$K_n(R_0 \times R_1) \cong K_n(R_0) \oplus K_n(R_1)$$
{{< /mathdef >}}

From the last two lemmas we can build a simple example:

{{< mathdef type="Example" name="$K_0$ of $\mathbb{R}\oplus \mathbb{Z}$" >}}
$K_0(\mathbb{R}\oplus \mathbb{Z}) \cong K_0(\mathbb{R}) \oplus K_0(\mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}\cong \mathbb{Z}^2.$
{{< /mathdef >}}

{{< mathdef type="Example" name="$K_0$ of $\mathbb{C}[Z/p]$" >}}
To calculate $K_0(\mathbb{C}[\mathbb{Z}/p])$ we must first notice $\mathbb{C}[\mathbb{Z}/p] \cong \mathbb{C}^p$:
$$K_0(\mathbb{C}[\mathbb{Z}/p]) \cong K_0(\mathbb{C}) \oplus K_0(\mathbb{C}) \oplus \cdots \oplus K_0(\mathbb{C}) \cong \mathbb{Z}^p.$$
{{< /mathdef >}}

We introduce now a couple of more concrete lemmas:

{{< mathdef type="Theorem" name="Rim" >}}
Let $p$ be a prime number. then
$$\begin{aligned}K_0(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/p]) &\cong K_0(\mathbb{Z}[e^{2\pi i / p}])\\
1_{\mathbb{Z}/p} &\mapsto e^{2\pi i / p}\end{aligned}$$
{{< /mathdef >}}

## The reduced projective class group

The reduced projective class group is considerably more interesting when working with algebraic topology. Due to its relation with the projective class group it can be easily defined as follows:

{{< mathdef type="Definition" name="Reduced projective class group" >}}
Let $R$ be a ring. We define $\tilde{K}_0(R)$ as the quotient of $K_0(R)$ by the subgroup generated by the class of the free module $[R]$.
{{< /mathdef >}}

Notice $\tilde{K}_0(R)$ is the cokernel of the group homomorphism $K_0(\mathbb{Z}) \to K_0(R)$ given by $[\mathbb{Z}^n]\mapsto n \mapsto [R^n]$ (You can check this is well defined by using that $\mathbb{Z}$ is a PID).

It is clear then that for a finitely generated projective module $P$, $[P]\in\tilde{K}_0(R)$ is 0 if and only if $[P]+[0] = [0]$, that is, exists $n,m\in \mathbb{N}$ such that $P \oplus R^n \cong R^m$. If $P$ satisfies this property we say it is **stably free**.

### Calculation

Usually $\tilde{K}_0(R)$ is harder to compute than $K_0(R)$. We will provide a couple of lemmas that may help with that.

{{< mathdef type="Lemma" name="$K_0(RG)$ for group rings" >}}
Let $G$ be a group and $R$ be a commutative integral domain, then 
$${K}_0(RG) \cong \tilde{K}_0(RG) \oplus \mathbb{Z}.$$
{{< /mathdef >}}

{{< mathdef type="Theorem" name="Finite abelian groups with vanishing $\tilde{K_0}(\mathbb{Z}G)$" id="tildeK0FiniteAbelian" >}}
Let $G$ be a finite <strong>abelian</strong> group, then $\tilde{K}_0(\mathbb{Z}G) = 0$ if and only if one of the following conditions is satisfied:
<ul>
  <li> $G$ is cyclic of order $n$ for $1\leq n \leq 11$ or $n = 13,14,17, 19$;</li>
  <li> $G$ is $\mathbb{Z}/2 \oplus \mathbb{Z}/2$;</li>
</ul>
{{< /mathdef >}}

{{< mathdef type="Theorem" name="Finite non-abelian groups with vanishing $\tilde{K_0}(\mathbb{Z}G)$" >}}
Let $G$ be a finite <strong>non-abelian</strong> group, then $\tilde{K}_0(\mathbb{Z}G) = 0$ if and only if one of the following conditions is satisfied:
<ul>
  <li> $G$ is $D_{2n}$ for $n \geq 3$.</li>
  <li> $G$ is $A_4, A_5$ or $S_4$.</li>
</ul>
{{< /mathdef >}}

We can now make a few examples:

{{< mathdef type="Example" name="$K_0(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/2])$" >}}
$K_0(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/2]) \cong \tilde{K}_0(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/2]) \oplus \mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}$ as $\mathbb{Z}/2$ is cyclic of order 2.
{{< /mathdef >}}

# The first K group

We now define the first $k$-group, $K_1$. It is an "elevation" on the concept of $K_0$ in the sense that we are now working with $R$-morphisms instead of $R$-modules. We will see this is a difficult definition to work with and that is where $K$-groups as invariants of matrices will come into place.

## Definition

{{<mathdef type="Definition" name="$K_1$ group" >}}
Let $R$ be a ring. We define the $K_1$-group of a $R$ as the abelian group generated by the conjugacy clases $[f]$ of automorphisms $f:P\to P$ of finitely generated projective $R$-modules with sum defined as:

For any commutative diagram of finitely generated projective $R$-modules of the following form:

<img src="/blog/lower-K-Theory/images/sumk1.webp" style="width: 35%;" />

we have that
$$[f_2]=[f_1]+[f_3].$$

{{</mathdef>}}

This $K$-group, too, defines a functor $K_1(-)$ from the category of rings to the category of abelian groups by sending the ring homomorphism $f:R\to S$ to the group homomorphism

$$
\begin{align*}
K_1(f): K_1(R)&\to K_1(R)\\\\
(g:P\to P) & \mapsto (f_*g: f_*P\to f_*P)=g \otimes_R S.
\end{align\*}
$$


It can be shown that $[f_1\circ f_3]=[f_1] + [f_3]$ for $f_1,f_3:P_1\to P_1$ automorphisms of finitely generated projective $R$-modules. Indeed, consider the conmutative diagram

<img src= "/blog/lower-K-Theory/images/remarksumk1.webp" style="width: 35%;" />

then by $P_1, P_2$ being finitely generated projective $R$-modules, the sequences split and $P_2=P_1^2$ so
$$
f_2=\begin{pmatrix}
f_1 & \alpha\\\\
0 & f_3
\end{pmatrix}
$$
where $\alpha$ is an automorphism of $P_1$. We can then consider the automorphism 
$$h=\begin{pmatrix}
1 & (f_1-f_3)^{-1}\alpha\\\\
0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}$$

and it is easy to check that 
$$hf_2h^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix}
f_1 & 0\\\\
0 & f_3
\end{pmatrix}\sim \begin{pmatrix}
f_1\circ f_3 & 0\\\\
0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}\sim f_1\circ f_3$$

Where the first $\sim$ is a base change (by elementary matrices) in the ring of automorphisms of $P_1$.

It is nevertheless interesting to understand $K_1$ as a property of matrices of $R$.

{{ <mathdef type="Definition" name="Elementary group ($E(R)$)" > }}
Let $R$ be a ring, we say a matrix is $elementary$ if its of the form
$$Id_n+rE_n(i,j)$$
Where $r\in R$, $i\neq j\in \{1,\cdots, n\}$ and 
$$(E_n(i,j))_{n,m}=\begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } i\neq n \text{ or } j\neq m\\
1 & \text{if } i= n \text{ or } j=m.
\end{cases}$$
Let $E(R)\subset GL(R)$ be the multiplicative subgroup generated by elementary matrices.
{{ </mathdef> }}

You may have already noticed elementary matrices correspond with determinant preserving operations so $GL(R)/E(R)$ surjects via group homomorphism into $R^\times$ by the determinant, in particular $[A]=[B]$ if and only if $A$ can be obtaind from $B$ by elementary row and colum operations and by taking dirrect sums by $I$. This interests us because of the following theorem:

{{<mathdef type="Theorem" name="$K_1(R)$ equals $GL(R)/E(R)$">}}
For any ring $R$ we have that
$$K_1(R) \cong GL(R)/E(R).$$
{{</mathdef>}}

And in particular

{{<mathdef type="Lemma" name="$K_1(R)$ surjects into $R^\times$" id="K1Surjects" >}}
For any ring $R$ we have that
$$K_1(R) \xrightarrow{det} R^\times$$
describes a surjective group homomorphism.
{{</mathdef>}}

## The $NK_n$ groups and how to calculate them

Before continuing to the calculations of $K_1$ we are interested on studing the interaction between different $K$ groups. To archieve this we need first to define an auxiliary object.

{{<mathdef type="Definition" name="$NK_n$">}}
Let $R$ be a ring. For all $n\in\mathbb{Z}$ we define $NK_n(R)$ by
$$
NK_n(R):=\ker\left(K_n(R[t])\xrightarrow{K_n(ev_0)} K_n(R)\right)
$$
{{</mathdef>}}

This definition will be usefull for the Bass-Heller-Swan decomposition which will allow us to use different $K$ groups to help with computations. To this end it is usefull to know a couple of lemmas to help us compute $NK_n$.

{{<mathdef type="Theorem" name="Bass-Heller-Swan decomposition for $NK$ groups">}}
If $R$ is a regular ring, then

$$NK_n(R) = \{0\} \qquad \text{for } n \in \mathbb{Z};$$
{{</mathdef>}}

{{<mathdef type="Theorem" name="Vanishing of $NK_n(\mathbb{Z}[G \times \mathbb{Z}^k])$ for $n \le 1$, $k \ge 0$ and finite $G$ of square-free order">}}
Let $G$ be a finite group whose order is square-free. Then
$$
NK_n(\mathbb{Z}[G \times \mathbb{Z}^k]) = 0 \quad \text{for } n \le 1 \text{ and } k \ge 0.
$$
{{</mathdef>}}

## Calculation of $K_1$

As the Morita Theorem and the product for $K_n$ described in the projective group can be applied to any $n$ they can be applied to $K_1$. There are also other methods for calculating $K_1$ under certain conditions, for example, as a consequence of <a href="#K1Surjects">$K_1(R)$ surjecting into $R^\times$</a>, we have the following result:

{{<mathdef type="Lemma" name="$K_1(R)$ for R euclidean">}}
For any euclidean ring $R$ we have that
$$K_1(R) \xrightarrow{det} R^\times$$
describes a group isomorphism.
{{</mathdef>}}

Which gives us an easy way to calculate $K_1$:

{{<mathdef type="Example">}}
$K_1(\mathbb{Z}[i])\cong \{1, i, -1, -i\}\cong \mathbb{Z}/4$.
{{</mathdef>}}

But it is sometimes useful to use other $K$-groups to help with the calculation.

{{<mathdef type="Theorem" name="Bass-Heller-Swan decomposition for $K$ groups" id="Bass-Heller-Swan">}}
<ol>
<li>
There are isomorphisms of abelian groups, natural in $R$, for $n \in \mathbb{Z}$

$$
NK_n(R) \oplus K_n(R) \xrightarrow{\cong} K_n(R[t]);
$$

$$
K_n(R) \oplus K_{n-1}(R) \oplus NK_n(R) \oplus NK_n(R)
\xrightarrow{\cong} K_n(R[t,t^{-1}]).
$$
</li>

<li>
There is a sequence, which is natural in $R$ and exact, for $n \in \mathbb{Z}$

$$
0 \to K_n(R) 
\xrightarrow{K_n(k_+) \oplus -K_n(k_-)} 
K_n(R[t]) \oplus K_n(R[t^{-1}])
$$

$$\xrightarrow{K_n(l_+) \oplus K_n(l_-)}
K_n(R[t,t^{-1}])
\xrightarrow{c_n}
K_{n-1}(R) \to 0$$

where $k_+, k_-, l_+, $ and $l_-$ are the obvious inclusions.

If we regard it as an acyclic $\mathbb{Z}$-chain complex, there exists a chain contraction,
natural in $R$.
</li>
</ol>

{{</mathdef>}}

We can now use the Bass-Heller-Swan theorem to calculate an example

{{<mathdef type="Example">}}
Consider $K_1(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/5 \times \mathbb{Z}])$ and assume we can calculate $K_1(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/5])$. We can rewrite the desired group by $K_1(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/5][t, t^{-1}])$ which, by Bass-Heller-Swan theorem 
$$K_1(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/5][t, t^{-1}])\cong K_1(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/5])\oplus K_0(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/5])$$
$$\oplus NK_1(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/5]) \oplus NK_1(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/5]).$$
As $\mathbb{Z}/5$ is a finite group of square free order, we know $NK_1(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/5])=0$ so 
$$K_1(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/5][t, t^{-1}])\cong K_1(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/5])\oplus K_0(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/5]).$$
Because $\mathbb{Z}/5$ is a finite group of order $5<11$ we know that $K_0(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/5])=\mathbb{Z}$ so 
$$K_1(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/5][t, t^{-1}])\cong K_1(\mathbb{Z}[\mathbb{Z}/5])\oplus \mathbb{Z}.$$
And we have reached our objective.
{{</mathdef>}}

There is still one final result relating $K$ groups of diferent dimensions:

{{<mathdef type="Theorem" name="Mayer-Vietoris sequence for 𝐾-theory in degree ≤ 2" id="Mayer-Vietoris">}}
Consider a pullback of rings
<img src="/blog/lower-K-Theory/images/mayervietoris.webp" style="width: 15%;" />
such that $j_1$ and $j_2$ are surjective, then the following sequence is exact:
$$
\begin{aligned}
K_2(R) & \to K_2(R_1) \oplus K_2(R_2) \to K_2(R_0)\\\\
\to K_1(R) & \to K_1(R_1) \oplus K_1(R_2) \to K_1(R_0)\\\\
\to K_0(R) & \to K_0(R_1) \oplus K_0(R_2) \to K_0(R_0) \\\\
\to K_{-1}(R) & \to K_{-1}(R_1) \oplus K_{-1}(R_2) \to K_{-1}(R_0) \to \cdots
\end{aligned}
$$
{{</mathdef>}}

## Whitehead group

It is now natural to consider what the reduced $K_1$ group $\tilde{K}_1$ would look like. We can define it simetrically to how we define the reduced $K_0$ group, that is, as the cokernel of the group homomorphism
$$
\begin{align*}
K_1(\mathbb{Z})\cong \{1,-1\}&\to K_1(R)\\\\
a &\mapsto [(a_R)]
\end{align*}
$$
Where $[(a)]$ denotes the class of the $(1,1)$-matrix $(a)$. This is the basis for the definition we will be providing

{{<mathdef type="Definition" name="Reduced $K_1$ group, $\tilde{K}_1$" >}}
Let $R$ be a ring. We define $\tilde{K}_1(R)$ as the quotient of $K_1(R)$ by the cyclic subgroup of at most order two generated by the class of the $(1, 1)$-matrix $(−1_R)$.
{{</mathdef>}}

We can extend this definition to a functor $Wh(-)$ from the category of groups to the category of abelian groups by taking the quotient 
$$
\begin{align*}
Wh(G):&=\tilde{K}_1(\mathbb{Z}G)/\\{(g) \ | \ g\in G\\}\\\\
&=coker \left(\begin{align*}
G\times \\{+1,-1\\} &\to K_1(\mathbb{Z}G)\\\\
(g,\pm1) & \mapsto [(\pm g)]
\end{align*}\right).
\end{align*}$$

We can now define the Whitehead group

{{<mathdef type="Definition" name="Whitehead group, $Wh(G)$" >}}
Let $G$ be a group. We define $Wh(G)$ as the quotient of $K_1(\mathbb{Z}G)$ by the subgroup comprised by the class of the $(1, 1)$-matrices $(g)$ with $g\in G$.
{{</mathdef>}}
